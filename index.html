EV Charging an Overview

As BHARAT is beginning to make the transition to Electric Vehicles (EVs), there seems to be a lot of confusion surrounding the new technology packed into them, unsurprising when you consider that, fundamentally, vehicle propulsion has remained largely unchanged for well over 100 years.

While similar in appearance to their traditional internal combustion engine (ICE) powered counterparts, EVs are completely different animals. There are a few important concepts to get your head around to properly understand EVs, the first is the capacity of the battery, this is measured in kilowatt hours (KW/H), and is analogous to liters when discussing the fuel capacity of an ICE vehicle, it is the amount of energy that the vehicle can store.

The second important measure to understand is efficiency, traditionally this is measured in liters per 100 kilometers, perfectly fine, for a liquid fuel, and the EV equivalent is kilometers per kilowatt hour. So its easy to see that given these two pieces of information, a simple calculation of battery capacity (KW/H) divided by efficiency (KM per KW/H) is how range for an EV is calculated, of course real world range may vary from this for a number of factors.

Charging Types

3 main charging types exist for EVs and they are categorized as follows:

Level 1– these are the most basic chargers that many of us with EVs would use at home, they have a standard 10 AMP or in some cases a 15 AMP plug. They are compact and convenient, but due to the limited power available from socket outlets, they are very slow, usually having outputs in the range of 2 or 3 KW AC, meaning charge times of many hours or even multiple days depending on the vehicle.

Level 2– these are a big step up from level one chargers in terms of power and therefore speed. They are common in businesses and at public charging stations, some may even be lucky enough to have enough power, and not to mention off street parking, to allow the installation of one of these units at home. Level 2 chargers are usually hard wired, with inputs ranging from 16-32 amps, and outputs ranging from around 7kw up to 22kw AC, however anything larger than the 7KW models will usually require a 3 phase, 415V supply, something most homes will not have access to. The largest 22KW models can charge certain vehicles in just 3-4 hours.

Level 3- these are the big dogs of charging, they range in outputs from 50 up to 350KW, and this is a huge amount of power. These chargers are very large and heavy, requiring hundreds of AMPs of input current, and easily costing 6 figures. For these reasons, they are not suited to home charging, but usually found at public charging stations. Their outputs are also DC unlike the aforementioned level 1 and 2 chargers, and can often fully charge vehicles in well under an hour.

Alternating Current vs Direct Current

No, we are not discussing the current wars of the 1890s, but rather the impact of this difference on EV charging today. While I said that level 2 charging can charge at up to 22KW, there is a caveat to this, a 22KW charger may only be able to charge certain vehicles at 11 or even 7KW. Why? Well the output of these chargers is AC, but your cars system relies on storing energy in batteries which operate with DC, so it is necessary to have a converter between the AC input from the charger, and the vehicles batteries. Therefore, more premium models such as some from tesla or BYD can accept the full 22KW AC power output of the charger, were as more budget brands may only allow 7KW. There are a number of reasons for this, such as cost obviously, but also weight and heat dissipation. So before installing chargers, it is worth checking your vehicles capacity.

Level 3 DC chargers, or fast chargers as they are also known do all of this work for the vehicle, and put power straight into the cars batteries, the only limit on DC charging is usually the battery packs construction and ability to dissipate the heat of fast charging. Nearly all new vehicles can charge at 50KW DC or more, again some premium models are capable of accepting 300KW+ charging power.
